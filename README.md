## 🔬 Featured Research

### 1. The Childcare Constraint
*Estimating the causal effect of childcare access on labor supply in Central Minnesota* — **Working paper, preparing for submission**

Twenty years of MN DHS/DCYF childcare licensing records matched to ACS PUMS
microdata (N ≈ 103,000), analyzed with DiD/TWFE, IV, and RD at the CCAP
subsidy-eligibility threshold. Key result: labor supply responses diverge
sharply by household type, and full-time work shows a large discontinuity at
the subsidy cliff — how assistance phases out matters as much as how much
childcare exists.
→ [Code](https://github.com/PBaffoe88/Economics/tree/main/ChildCare-labor-Supply)

### 2. Buffer Depletion and Price Instability
*The inventory channel of monetary policy in U.S. manufacturing* — **Working paper**

Jordà local projections with two-way fixed effects. After a contractionary
monetary shock, inventory-intensive sectors show a persistent rise in
inventory-to-shipments ratios (elevated ~18 months) and higher producer price
volatility peaking around six months post-shock; a 12-month placebo shock shows
no comparable pattern.
→ [Code](https://github.com/PBaffoe88/Economics/tree/main/Monetary-Policy-Inventory_Channel)

### 3. Oil Prices and Geopolitical Risk
*What 35 years of data say about crude oil and geopolitical uncertainty* — **Working paper + published article**

Markov-switching, EGARCH, and local projection models using the
Caldara–Iacoviello GPR Index. Geopolitical risk manifests as volatility and
regime-switching — not the persistent price spikes conventional wisdom
predicts.
→ [Code](https://github.com/PBaffoe88/Economics/tree/main/CrudeOil-Momentum-GeoP-Risk) ·
[LinkedIn article](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/what-35-years-data-say-oil-prices-geopolitical-risk-paul-danso-qnvvc/)

### 4. Spillover Effects of U.S. Fiscal Policy on OECD Monetary Policy
*M.S. thesis — St. Cloud State University*

Two-stage design: a Cholesky-identified SVAR extracts U.S. structural shocks;
country-level ARDL models trace policy-rate responses across 30+ OECD
economies. High-income economies tend to loosen in response to a U.S. fiscal
stimulus shock, while other OECD economies tend to tighten.

### 5. Order Cadence Model
*Industry project — Bay State Milling*

A quantitative procurement decision-support framework integrating demand
forecasts, inventory position, safety stock, and lead-time uncertainty.
Improved planning accuracy by ~25%.
